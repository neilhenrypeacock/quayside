# Quayside — Marketing Strategy

> This is the single source of truth for all Quayside messaging, positioning, and tone. Every page on the site, every outreach email, every social post, and every conversation with a port or merchant should draw from this document. If copy doesn't align with what's in here, it's wrong.

---

## 1. THE PROBLEM

The UK fish market is fragmented. Prices vary significantly from port to port, day to day — but most buyers only ever see their corner of it.

A merchant in Grimsby doesn't know that cod was 18% cheaper in Peterhead this morning. A processor locked into one port relationship doesn't know they've been overpaying versus the network average for months. A restaurant buyer has no way to check whether their supplier's prices are fair.

The information exists. It's just scattered across six different port websites, PDF price sheets, Excel downloads, and phone calls to contacts at the docks. Some ports don't publish at all. The government publishes retrospective data, but it's quarterly at best — useless for daily buying decisions.

**The result:** most merchants buy from the port they know, pay whatever the price is that day, and have no way to tell if they got a good deal or a bad one. The ones with the best phone networks and the most time have an advantage. Everyone else flies blind.

**This is the status quo Quayside replaces.**

---

## 2. THE SOLUTION

Quayside aggregates fish auction prices from ports across the UK into one place and delivers them to buyers every day.

That's it. No complexity. No jargon. One place to see what fish is selling for, everywhere.

For the first time, a merchant can open their phone in the morning and see prices across the whole UK market before they buy. They can spot where haddock is cheapest, track how monkfish prices have moved over the past month, and know whether the price they're being quoted is fair.

The information comes to them. They don't have to chase it.

## 5. TARGET AUDIENCES

### Primary: Independent Fish Merchants (Trade tier — £95/month)
- **Who they are:** Buy at auction across one or more ports, sell to restaurants, fishmongers, and food service. Typically owner-operators or small teams. Age 35–60. Practical, busy, phone-first. Not especially online. Value relationships and trust over flashy tools.
- **What they do today:** Mix of checking individual port websites, calling contacts at ports, and just buying from their usual port because they don't have time to check everywhere.
- **What Quayside gives them:** The whole market in one place, every morning. They can see where the best prices are, compare across ports, and spot opportunities they'd never have found manually.
- **What makes them pay:** The knowledge that they're not overpaying — and that they're seeing ports they wouldn't otherwise check. £95/month is nothing if it saves them even one bad buying decision a week.
- **Emotional outcome:** Calm. In control. "The information comes to me."

### Secondary: Processors (Trade tier — £95/month)
- **Who they are:** Buy in bulk (whole species, large volumes), fillet/pack for retail. Bigger operations, more analytical, often have procurement teams.
- **What Quayside gives them:** Cross-port price tracking to optimise procurement. Historical trends to inform forward buying. Visibility into supply patterns across ports.
- **What makes them pay:** Margin optimisation on high-volume purchases. Even small per-kilo savings compound at scale.

### Secondary: Fish Wholesalers / Distributors (Trade tier — £95/month)
- **Who they are:** Middlemen between ports and end buyers. Buy across multiple ports, resell regionally. Need broad market visibility to arbitrage.
- **What Quayside gives them:** The widest cross-port view. Spread analysis. The ability to spot where a species is cheap in one port and expensive in another.
- **What makes them pay:** Their entire business model depends on information advantage. Quayside is their intelligence layer.

### Future: Restaurant Buyers / Head Chefs (lower tier)
- **Not the launch audience.** Park this for later. The trade audience pays more, has clearer pain, and is closer to the data.

### Separate audience: Port Operators
- **Messaging lives on `/for-ports` only — not in main marketing.**
- **Their motivation:** More buyers seeing their prices = more competition at auction = better outcomes for their sellers.
- **Quayside's pitch to them:** Free visibility to a national audience of fish buyers. No cost, no risk, no commitment.

## 7. TONE OF VOICE

### Personality
Quayside sounds like **a sharp, trusted contact in the trade** — someone who knows the market inside out, gives you the facts straight, and doesn't waste your time with waffle.

Two defining traits:
1. **Trustworthy / reliable** — We show up every morning with accurate data. No hype, no exaggeration, no fluff. If we say it, it's true.
2. **Sharp / precise** — We know our numbers. We're concise. We respect your time. Every word earns its place.

### The voice in practice

**We sound like:**
- A knowledgeable industry peer, not a salesperson
- Someone who's been at the market at 5am, not someone selling you software
- A well-informed trade publication, not a tech startup blog

**We are:**
- Direct — short sentences, clear language, no padding
- Specific — real numbers, real ports, real species names
- Honest — if we don't cover a port yet, we say so
- Grounded — we talk about fish, prices, and ports, not "data ecosystems"

**We are not:**
- Salesy or pushy — no "Act now!" or "Don't miss out!"
- Techy for the sake of it — we don't lead with AI, algorithms, or "our technology"
- Vague — no "unlock insights" or "leverage data-driven decisions"
- Preachy — we don't lecture the industry about how they should work

### Register
Somewhere between a trade magazine and a text message from a contact. Professional but not corporate. Friendly but not matey. We'd say "prices were up across the board" not "price indices demonstrated upward trajectory" — but we also wouldn't say "mental day at the market lads."

## 8. LANGUAGE RULES

### Words we use
| Instead of... | We say... |
|---|---|
| Platform | Quayside (or just "us") |
| Data points / records | Prices |
| Users / customers | Merchants, buyers, traders |
| Onboard / activate | Join, sign up, get started |
| Content / insights | Market intelligence, the numbers |
| Leverage / utilise | Use |
| Ecosystem | Network (of ports) |
| Stakeholders | Ports, merchants, buyers |
| Solution | — (just describe what it does) |
| Deliver value | Save money / save time / show you the market |

### Terms we use for what Quayside provides
- **Market intelligence** — the overarching term for the product
- **Market data** — the prices and trends themselves
- **Auction prices** — when being specific about what we aggregate
- **Daily digest** — the morning email/notification
- **Price intelligence** — when emphasising the analysis layer (trade audience)

### Terms we avoid
| Word/phrase | Why |
|---|---|
| Disrupt / disruptive | Overused tech speak. We're not disrupting — we're serving an underserved market. |
| Ecosystem | Too abstract. Say "network" or just name the ports. |
| Real-time | Not accurate — data is daily, not live. Say "daily" or "every morning." |
| Cutting-edge | Empty. Just describe what it does. |
| Unlock / unleash | Cliché startup language. |
| Best-in-class | Says nothing. |
| Empower | Patronising. Our audience doesn't need empowering — they need prices. |
| Seamless | Overused and vague. |
| End-to-end | Jargon. |
| Democratise | Wrong register entirely for this audience. |

### When to mention AI
- **In the product itself:** The AI chat feature is a selling point. Call it "AI chat" or "ask a question about the market." Don't hide it.
- **In marketing copy:** Don't lead with AI. Nobody buys Quayside because of AI — they buy it because it shows them prices. AI is the how, not the what.
- **Acceptable:** "Ask our AI chat anything about today's prices" / "Powered by AI extraction for ports that send us price sheets"
- **Not acceptable:** "AI-powered market intelligence platform" as a headline or primary descriptor

### When to mention technology
- Only when it builds trust (e.g., "We collect data directly from port auction systems")
- Never as the lead message
- The audience cares about the data, not how it's collected

## 10. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING

### What we replace

| What exists today | Limitation | What Quayside does instead |
|---|---|---|
| Individual port websites | One port at a time, different formats, some don't publish at all | All ports, one format, one place |
| Phone networks / contacts | Depends who you know, time-consuming, incomplete | Systematic, complete, every morning |
| Seafish / MMO / Defra data | Quarterly or annual, retrospective, useless for daily buying | Daily, forward-looking, actionable |
| Buying from your usual port | Can't see alternatives, no benchmark for fair price | Full market visibility, price benchmarks |
| Nothing (just accepting the price) | No leverage, no information, paying whatever it costs | Information that gives you leverage |

### How we talk about competition
- We don't name competitors or badmouth anyone
- We describe the status quo honestly: "Right now, there's no single place to see fish prices across UK ports"
- We acknowledge Seafish/MMO positively when relevant: "Great for annual trends, but not designed for daily buying decisions"
- We never claim to be "the only" or "the first" without qualification

## 11. MESSAGING DO'S AND DON'TS

### Do
- Lead with the benefit, not the feature ("See where haddock is cheapest" not "Our cross-port comparison tool")
- Use real species names, real port names, real numbers where possible
- Keep sentences short — if it's longer than 15 words, try splitting it
- Write at a reading age of 12–14 (trade audience, phone screens, scanning not reading)
- Test every headline by asking: "Would a merchant in Peterhead nod at this?"

### Don't
- Promise coverage or features you don't have yet
- Use stock photography of fish markets (use real data screenshots instead)
- Put "AI" or "technology" in a headline
- Use more than one exclamation mark per page (ideally zero)
- Write in the third person ("Quayside helps merchants...") when second person works ("See prices across...")
